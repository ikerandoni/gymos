Agility 🏃
Speed · Court drills · Blocking · 15 min before or after practice
When to do this. On court, before or after practice, 2–3x per week. Not in the gym. These drills train your nervous system for basketball — the gym builds the engine, this builds the car.

Speed Drills

Sprint Intervals
4×30 meters · full speed · 90s rest between
Drive hard from the first step — don't ease into it
Pump arms — your arms drive your legs
Full rest between reps — speed work is not cardio
Lane Agility Drill
4 corners of the paint · shuffle, sprint, backpedal · 4 rounds
Stay low — bend your knees, don't stand straight up
Quick feet — short fast steps, not big slow ones
Change direction on a dime — plant and push off hard

Defensive Drills

Defensive Slide
Side to side across the paint · 3×30 seconds
Low stance — stay below your opponent's center of gravity
Never cross your feet — slide and recover
Hands active — one low, one high
Closeout Drill
Sprint from paint to 3pt line, chop feet, contest shot · 5 reps
Sprint full speed until the last 2 steps
Chop steps to control momentum — don't fly past them
Hand up immediately — contest without fouling

Blocking + Timing Drills

Vertical Timing (wall taps)
Jump and tap a point on the wall · 3×10 · focus on timing
Read the shooter — jump when they go up, not before
Reach with one hand — more extension than two hands together
Stay vertical — don't lean into the shooter
Reaction Sprint
Partner calls L/R · you sprint that direction · 3×8
Start in defensive stance — already moving when you hear the call
First step is everything — explosive, not shuffling
No partner? Use a phone — random left/right on screen

Weekly Agility Plan

Monday/Wednesday/Friday — 15 min agility after practice or shoot-around.
Tuesday/Thursday — rest from court drills, gym is enough.
Saturday game day — warmup only, don't exhaust yourself before tip-off.
Knee check. If lateral movement causes sharp knee pain, stop immediately and do straight-line drills only.
Mobility 🧘
10 min · before bed or after training · protects knees + shoulders long-term
Why this matters for you specifically. Your meniscus, your shoulder health, and your long-term athletic career all depend on keeping the surrounding tissue mobile. 10 minutes a day beats an hour once a week.

Hip + Knee (do every day)

Hip Flexor Stretch
2×45 seconds each side
Kneeling lunge position — back knee on the floor
Tuck your pelvis under — don't just stand tall, squeeze the glute
Tight hip flexors pull your pelvis forward and put stress on your lower back and knee
90/90 Hip Rotation
2×30 seconds each side
Sit on the floor, both knees at 90 degrees, one in front one to the side
Sit tall, lean slightly over the front leg
Best hip rotation drill for basketball — improves lateral movement
Terminal Knee Extension (TKE)
2×15 each leg · band around a post
Band behind knee, step back to create tension
Straighten the knee against the band resistance — feel the VMO fire
This directly protects your meniscus — do it daily
Calf + Ankle Stretch
2×45 seconds each side
Wall stretch — heel flat, lean forward, feel the stretch
Ankle circles — 10 each direction
Tight calves restrict your jump and put stress on your knee — keep them loose

Shoulder + Upper body (gym days)

Band Pull-Apart
3×15 · light band · do before every push session
Arms straight out, pull band apart to chest level
Squeeze rear delts — this warms up the rotator cuff
Prevents shoulder impingement from heavy pressing over time
Chest Doorframe Stretch
2×30 seconds each side
Arm at 90 degrees on a doorframe, rotate body away
Feel the front of the chest and shoulder open up
Counters the forward shoulder posture from all the pressing
Thoracic Spine Rotation
10 each side · on all fours
On hands and knees, one hand behind head
Rotate elbow toward the sky — follow with your eyes
Upper back mobility directly improves your shot, your dribbling, and your posture

Winged Scapula (do daily)

Serratus Push-up Plus
3×15 · most important one · do every day
Get into a push-up position, arms straight at the top
Push your upper back toward the ceiling — spread the shoulder blades apart
Hold 2 seconds at the top, lower back to neutral — that's one rep
This directly trains the serratus anterior — the muscle that holds the scapula flat
Wall Slide
3×12 · arms flat against the wall the whole time
Stand with back flat against the wall, arms in a goalpost position
Slowly slide arms overhead — keep wrists, elbows, and upper back touching the wall
If anything lifts off the wall, stop there and work that range
Trains the lower trap and serratus together — the two muscles that fix winging
Prone Y Raise
3×12 · lie face down · thumbs up
Lie face down on a bench or the floor, arms out in a Y shape, thumbs pointing up
Raise your arms off the floor — feel the lower traps squeeze
Hold 2 seconds at the top, lower with control
Lower traps are the other key muscle keeping your scapula flat — most people never train them directly
Dead Hang
30–60 seconds · end of every pull session
Hang from a pull-up bar, arms straight, let gravity do the work
Relax the shoulders — don't shrug or engage anything
Decompresses the shoulder joint and naturally resets scapular position
Also great for grip strength and shoulder health long term
What causes it. Weak serratus anterior and lower traps — very common from doing lots of pressing without balancing it with scapular stability work. Fully fixable in a few months of consistent daily work.
Heavy pressing. Don't max out on bench while the winging is bad — the shoulder joint takes load it shouldn't when the scapula isn't stable. Keep pressing but be aware of it.

Hamstring + Lower back

Standing Hamstring Stretch
2×45 seconds each side
Foot on a bench or chair, leg straight
Hinge at the hip — don't round the back
Tight hamstrings pull on the lower back and knee — stretch them daily
Cat-Cow
10 slow reps · before bed
On hands and knees, breathe in as you arch your back (cow)
Breathe out as you round your spine up (cat)
Takes 60 seconds, decompresses the spine after heavy lifting
